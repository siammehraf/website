✧ My Key Takeaways from "The Philosophy of Professional Writing" by Brandon Sanderson -

• We will receive bad advice from almost anyone, including popular and successful writers. Some advice will work for us, and some won’t. That’s normal.

• Orson Scott Card warns that writing without an outline will destroy your story and turn it into a disaster. On the other hand, Stephen King says in his book _On Writing_ that outlines can ruin the creative process. Can we do both? **Yes, we can.**

• Sometimes when we tell people about our story, they immediately ask, “When are you publishing it?” and similar questions. It’s completely okay for them to ask, but publishing (or not publishing) should **not** be the main reason we write. We should write because writing is good for us. Creating art is good for us.

• If you haven’t published at least three to five books yet, your main purpose is not selling books — even if you eventually want to become a professional writer. Right now, your focus should be finding **your own writing process**. Only after you’ve developed that process can you seriously think about selling your work.

• Sometimes an idea feels gorgeous and perfect in our imagination, but when we write it down, it turns ugly and doesn’t go where we wanted or expected. This usually means our skills are not yet equal to our aspirations. With consistent time and practice, our skills will catch up.

• While writing, we are in control — we make the decisions for the story and characters. But once the book is finished, we need to put “the artist” back in the closet and shut the door.

• Revision is a difficult but extremely important skill to learn. Brandon Sanderson says (from his own experience) that writers who finish a chapter and then keep revising it over and over again often fail to complete the book.

• What motivated him in the beginning? He set a clear daily goal and tried hard to finish it. When he wrote, time flew by without him noticing. That doesn’t mean writing is easy, but on most days he ended up doubling his goal. He recommends setting a goal that is a little difficult but not impossible — such as **1,000 words per day** — and tracking your progress with a spreadsheet.

• If a story turns out bad in the first draft, keep it. You can’t just throw it away. You can rewrite it later. Rewriting is a hard skill, but one worth learning.
